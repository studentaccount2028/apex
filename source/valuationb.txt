Term
Definition
Comparable Companies Analysis (Comps)
A relative valuation method that estimates a company's value by benchmarking it against similar publicly traded peers using standardized multiples.
Enterprise Value (EV)
Market capitalization plus net debt plus preferred equity plus minority interest. Represents the total value of the business to all capital providers.
Equity Value
Market capitalization — share price times diluted shares outstanding. Represents the value of the business to equity holders only.

Enterprise value divided by EBITDA. The most widely used capital structure-neutral operating multiple in investment banking.

Enterprise value divided by revenue. Used for high-growth or pre-profitability companies where EBITDA is negative or distorted.
EV/EBIT
Enterprise value divided by EBIT. More accurate than EV/EBITDA for capital-intensive businesses where D&A is a real economic cost.
Price/Earnings (P/E)
Share price divided by earnings per share. The most intuitive but most manipulable equity multiple.
Price/Book (P/B)
Share price divided by book value per share. Used primarily for financial institutions where book value reflects earning assets.
Forward Multiple
A multiple based on projected (next twelve months or next fiscal year) earnings or EBITDA rather than historical figures.
LTM (Last Twelve Months)
The most recent trailing twelve-month period. The standard historical time period for comps analysis.
NTM (Next Twelve Months)
The projected forward twelve-month period. Used for forward multiples based on consensus estimates.
Precedent Transactions
A relative valuation method using multiples implied by completed M&A deals rather than public market trading values.
Control Premium
The incremental value paid above a target's standalone public market trading price to acquire a controlling interest. Typically 20–40% in strategic acquisitions.
Football Field Chart
A horizontal bar chart summarizing valuation outputs from multiple methodologies. The standard deliverable for communicating a valuation range to senior bankers and clients.
Peer Group
The set of publicly traded companies selected as comparables for a comps analysis. Selection requires business model, size, growth, and margin comparability.
Calendarization
The process of adjusting a company's financial figures from its fiscal year-end to a calendar year-end for consistent peer comparison.
Scrubbing
The process of adjusting raw financial data for non-recurring items to arrive at a normalized, apples-to-apples comparable figure.
Implied Equity Value
The valuation conclusion expressed in terms of per-share value, derived by applying a peer median or selected multiple to the subject company's metrics.

Section 1.1 — The Two Ways to Value Anything
Situation
A first-year analyst is given a sell-side assignment: determine a fair value range for a mid-sized industrial manufacturer being considered for acquisition. She builds a DCF. She arrives at $48/share. The company trades at $37/share. Is it cheap? Is her DCF wrong? Is the market missing something?
Her MD doesn't answer the question by looking at her DCF again. He asks: "What does the peer group trade at?" The analyst realizes she has answered the intrinsic value question but has not yet answered the relative value question. Both answers matter.
Two Frameworks, One Picture
There are fundamentally two ways to value any asset.
Intrinsic valuation asks: what is this asset worth on its own, based on the cash flows it will generate? The DCF is the canonical example. It ignores the market entirely and estimates value from first principles — forecasting cash flows, discounting them, and arriving at an independent conclusion.
Relative valuation asks: what does the market pay for similar assets right now? Comps analysis, precedent transactions, and multiples-based approaches all belong to this family. They observe what buyers and sellers are paying for comparable businesses and apply those implied prices to the subject company.
Neither approach is superior. They answer different questions. A DCF tells you what a business is theoretically worth. A comps table tells you what the market is currently paying for businesses that look like it. The tension between those two numbers — the gap between intrinsic value and market price — is where investment theses live.
l-callout block: Label: THE ANALYST MINDSET
"Every investment banking valuation uses both approaches. DCF tells you intrinsic value. Comps tell you where the market is. When DCF > comps, the stock may be cheap — or your DCF assumptions may be too optimistic. When comps > DCF, the market may be pricing in growth you haven't modeled — or the market may be wrong. Your job is not to pick one. Your job is to understand why they diverge."
Why Multiples Exist
A multiple is a compressed ratio that captures in a single number the relationship between a company's price and one of its financial metrics. It exists because of a simple behavioral reality: markets are moved by participants comparing values, and comparison requires standardization.
When a sophisticated investor looks at a technology company, they cannot intuitively process whether $14.7 billion is a reasonable price. But they can immediately process that the company trades at 22x next year's EBITDA — and compare that to a peer group that trades at 18x–25x. That comparison creates an anchor. The multiple makes the abstract price meaningful by expressing it relative to something fundamental.
This is why multiples dominate deal work. When Blackstone is evaluating a leveraged buyout, they need to quickly assess whether the seller's asking price is reasonable relative to what the market pays for comparable assets. When Goldman is pitching a company on an IPO, they frame the offering price in terms of multiples that are comparable to recent transactions and public peers. The multiple is the common language of deal pricing.

THE FUNDAMENTAL MULTIPLE STRUCTURE

                    Price (or Enterprise Value)
Multiple   =  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
                    Fundamental Metric (EBITDA, Revenue, EPS...)

The multiple answers: "How many dollars does the market pay per dollar of [metric]?"

A company trading at 12x EBITDA:
  → Buyers are paying $12 for every $1 of EBITDA the company generates
  → The inverse: EBITDA yield = 1/12 = 8.3%

The multiple is a price. It reflects:
  → Growth expectations (higher growth = higher multiple)
  → Risk perception (higher risk = lower multiple)
  → Capital intensity (heavy CapEx = lower multiple)
  → Competitive moat (durable advantage = higher multiple)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The Limitation You Must Understand From Day One
Multiples are only as good as the peer group you compare them against. An 18x EBITDA multiple is expensive if the peer group trades at 10x. It is cheap if the peer group trades at 25x. The multiple means nothing in isolation — it only means something relative to a relevant benchmark. This is why the single most important analytical judgment in comps analysis is not the arithmetic. It is the selection of the peer group.
A second, deeper limitation: multiples inherit the market's errors. If the market is pricing an entire sector expensively (as it did in technology in 2021 and in real estate in 2006), a comps table will tell you what comparable companies are worth to the market — not necessarily what they are worth fundamentally. This is why sophisticated analysts always cross-reference multiples with DCF, never rely on comps alone, and explicitly flag when the market appears to be in a regime of broad mispricing.

quiz-wrap | Label: KNOWLEDGE CHECK — LESSON 1
Question 1 — Comprehension: A DCF for Company X produces an intrinsic value of $55/share. The comps table implies a value range of $70–$82/share. The stock trades at $67/share. Which statement best describes the analytical interpretation?
A) The DCF must be wrong — the market is always right B) The stock appears cheap on DCF but is trading at a discount to comps; the divergence demands investigation into whether the DCF assumptions are conservative or the market is mispricing the company C) The stock is clearly expensive because it trades above DCF value D) No meaningful conclusion can be drawn
Answer: B | Explanation: When a DCF implies a lower value than comps, it typically means one of three things: the DCF is using conservative growth or margin assumptions, the peer group is overvalued (pulling up the comps range), or the market sees strategic value not captured in a standalone DCF. The analyst's job is to diagnose the divergence — not to accept either number uncritically. The stock trading between the two ranges is a meaningful setup for further analysis.

Question 2 — Application: Which of the following best explains why multiples are widely used in investment banking despite their limitations?
A) They are mathematically more precise than DCF B) They standardize comparison across companies, making abstract prices meaningful relative to fundamental metrics — the common language of deal pricing C) They eliminate the need for fundamental analysis D) Banks are required by regulators to use multiples
Answer: B | Explanation: Multiples exist because they solve a communication and comparison problem. Converting a $14.7 billion enterprise value to "22x NTM EBITDA" creates an immediately comparable benchmark. They are not more precise than DCF — they are more communicable and faster to compute, which is why they dominate pitch books and deal discussions. Their precision depends entirely on the quality of the peer group selection.

Section 2.1 — The Multiple Taxonomy
Every multiple belongs to one of two families: enterprise value multiples and equity value multiples. This distinction matters more than any other in comps analysis.
Enterprise value multiples use EV in the numerator and a metric available to all capital providers — revenue, EBITDA, EBIT, unlevered free cash flow — in the denominator. Because EV includes both equity and debt, these multiples are capital structure-neutral. You can compare a heavily leveraged company against a debt-free company on an apples-to-apples basis.
Equity value multiples use market capitalization (or price per share) in the numerator and a metric that flows to equity holders — net income, book equity, dividends — in the denominator. Because they sit below interest expense, these multiples are affected by how much debt the company carries. A company that borrows heavily to fund operations will show a depressed P/E even if its operating performance is identical to a debt-free peer. Using equity multiples to compare companies with different capital structures is the most common error junior analysts make.

Section 2.2 — EV/EBITDA: The Workhorse
EV/EBITDA is the primary multiple in investment banking for most industries. Understanding why requires understanding what EBITDA represents.
EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization — is an approximation of a company's pre-investment operating cash generation. It strips out: interest (financing decision), taxes (jurisdiction and structure), depreciation and amortization (non-cash, accounting-driven). What remains is the cash-like operational profit available before the company invests in maintaining and growing its asset base.
The reason EV/EBITDA dominates IB practice is that it enables the purest operational comparison across companies with different capital structures, tax rates, and accounting choices around D&A.
l-example block: Label: EV/EBITDA — WHY IT BEATS P/E FOR CROSS-CAPITAL-STRUCTURE COMPARISON
Two industrial companies, identical operations: Company A: No debt. Net income = $100M. Market cap = $1,500M. Company B: $800M debt at 5%. Net income = $60M. EV = $1,500M.
P/E comparison: Company A P/E: $1,500M / $100M = 15.0x ← Looks expensive Company B P/E: $840M equity / $60M = 14.0x ← Looks cheaper
EV/EBITDA comparison (both have $150M EBITDA): Company A: $1,500M / $150M = 10.0x Company B: $1,500M / $150M = 10.0x ← Identical, as it should be
Conclusion: P/E creates a misleading picture because debt artificially reduces the denominator (net income) while leaving the market cap denominator incomparable. EV/EBITDA strips out financing effects and reveals that the two companies are priced identically — which is economically correct since their operations are identical.
When EV/EBITDA is the right choice:
Most industrial, consumer, healthcare, and technology companies
Any comparison across companies with meaningfully different leverage
LBO analysis (buyers evaluate EV/EBITDA entry multiples against historical deal comps)
Whenever D&A policies differ significantly across peers
When EV/EBITDA has limitations:
Very capital-intensive businesses where D&A is a real cash drain (utilities, telecom): adding back D&A overstates free cash generation; use EV/EBIT or EV/FCFF
Companies with significant differences in CapEx intensity: EBITDA doesn't account for the cost of sustaining the asset base; EV/FCFF is more appropriate
Negative EBITDA companies: the multiple becomes meaningless or negative; use EV/Revenue

Section 2.3 — EV/Revenue: When Profitability Doesn't Exist Yet
EV/Revenue is the standard multiple for high-growth companies that are pre-profitability or have highly distorted EBITDA margins. In software, SaaS, biotech, and early-stage technology, EBITDA is often negative or near zero as companies reinvest aggressively. Using EV/EBITDA produces negative multiples or outliers that break any comps table.
EV/Revenue sidesteps the problem by focusing on the top line, which is almost always positive and growing even when profitability is distant. The tradeoff is that EV/Revenue says nothing about operating quality, cost discipline, or path to profitability. Two companies at 8x EV/Revenue might be radically different businesses: one burning cash at 30% of revenue, another near profitability.
l-formula block:
EV/REVENUE APPLICATION FRAMEWORK
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Use EV/Revenue when:
  → EBITDA is negative or near zero (pre-profitability growth stage)
  → EBITDA margins vary so widely across peers that EV/EBITDA
     produces incomparable outliers (e.g., some peers reinvesting
     heavily while others are mature)
  → Valuing high-growth SaaS, biotech, or internet businesses

Section 2.4 — P/E: The Most Famous but Most Dangerous Multiple
P/E is the most widely cited valuation metric in financial media and equity research. It is also the most frequently misused. A thorough analyst understands both why P/E is intuitive and why it requires significant caution in cross-company comparisons.
P/E = Market Capitalization / Net Income = Share Price / Earnings Per Share
The intuition is simple: if you pay 20x earnings for a stock, you are paying $20 for every $1 of annual profit. The lower the P/E, the cheaper the stock — in theory.
The problems arise from everything that sits between EBIT and net income: interest expense (which reflects leverage, not operations), taxes (which reflect jurisdiction and planning), and one-time items (restructuring, impairments, gains on asset sales). Two companies with identical operating businesses but different capital structures will have different P/E ratios. The company with more debt pays more interest, which reduces net income and makes P/E artificially high. The company with a lower tax rate looks cheaper than its operating fundamentals justify.

Section 2.5 — LTM vs. NTM: Historical or Forward
Every multiple must specify what period's financial figures it uses. The choice matters significantly for interpretation.
LTM (Last Twelve Months) uses the most recently completed twelve-month period — calculated as the prior full fiscal year plus the most recent available quarter(s) minus the same quarter(s) from the prior year. LTM is backward-looking and reflects actual, audited (or reported) financial performance.
NTM (Next Twelve Months) uses the forward projected twelve-month period — typically based on consensus Street estimates for the coming year. NTM is forward-looking and reflects expected performance. Most sell-side models price stocks on NTM multiples because the market is pricing expectations, not history.
In practice:
LTM multiples are used when comparing historical deal prices (precedent transactions are almost always quoted on LTM metrics at time of deal announcement)
NTM multiples are used when the subject company is in a transition period and LTM figures don't represent the ongoing business
Both are shown in a well-built comps table, labeled clearly
l-callout block: Label: THE ANALYST MINDSET
"When I present multiples, I always show LTM and NTM side by side. If the NTM multiple is much lower than LTM, it means the Street expects significant earnings growth — check whether those estimates are credible. If NTM is close to LTM, the business is mature or the Street expects flat performance. The ratio between LTM and NTM multiples tells you a story about growth expectations that the multiples alone don't communicate."

Question 1 — Comprehension: You are comparing two healthcare companies. Company A has $500M of net debt. Company B is debt-free. Both generate $200M of EBITDA. Which multiple is most appropriate for an apples-to-apples comparison?

Question 3 — Judgment: A company was acquired in a leveraged buyout 18 months ago and is being sold today. The LBO significantly increased its debt load but the operations improved substantially year-over-year. Which period — LTM or NTM — is more meaningful for valuing this company in a sale process?
A) LTM — historical figures are more reliable than projections B) NTM — the forward period better captures the improved run-rate trajectory C) Both should be presented, with NTM given more weight given the improving trajectory D) Neither — the LBO changed the business too much for any comparables to work

However, an improved trajectory argues for giving NTM greater emphasis — buyers are purchasing future cash flows, not historical ones. Section 3.1 — The Architecture of a Comps Table
A comps table — formally, a Comparable Companies Analysis or "public comps" — is a structured table that benchmarks a subject company against a peer group on standardized financial metrics and derived valuation multiples. It is one of the two or three most common outputs in any investment banking engagement.
Every comps table, regardless of sector, follows the same structural architecture:

Section 3.2 — Peer Group Selection: The Art Behind the Science
Selecting the peer group is the highest-judgment task in comps analysis. It is also the most frequently manipulated. Lawyers, sellers, and aggressive bankers know that the choice of comparables directly drives the implied valuation range — so the temptation to include high-multiple peers (to support a high valuation) or low-multiple peers (to argue something is cheap) is real. An analyst's credibility depends on making defensible, honest selections.
The four criteria for peer group selection, applied in order:
Criterion 1: Business Model Comparability (Primary Screen) The peer must be in the same industry and operate a sufficiently similar business model. Same product category, same customer type, same value chain position. A SaaS company and a legacy enterprise software company on perpetual license models are not true comps even though both sell software. A regional bank and a global investment bank are not comps even though both are "banks." Business model first.
Criterion 2: Financial Profile Comparability (Secondary Screen) Revenue scale, EBITDA margin, and growth rate should be in a similar range. A $200M revenue company with 15% margins should not be compared against a $20B revenue company with 35% margins. Different scale creates different operational leverage, pricing power, and capital market access. The multiples will be structurally different for reasons unrelated to valuation.
Criterion 3: Geographic Comparability A U.S.-listed company should primarily be compared against U.S. peers. Cross-border comps are used when no domestic peers exist, but require adjustments for different tax regimes, growth environments, and multiple conventions. European companies consistently trade at lower multiples than U.S. counterparts in the same sectors — including this without adjustment will artificially suppress the implied valuation range.
Criterion 4: Availability and Quality of Financial Data The peer must have at minimum two years of reported financials, current Street consensus estimates, and reasonably clean financials (no ongoing accounting investigations, recently emerged from bankruptcy, etc.). Private companies and lightly traded micro-caps are generally excluded.
l-callout block: Label: THE ANALYST MINDSET — THE PEER GROUP TEST
"I apply a simple test to every company I consider for a peer group: if the company being valued and this proposed peer were both in a room with the same buyer, would the buyer evaluate them as alternatives? If the answer is yes — same customer base, same competitive dynamics, same growth drivers — it belongs. If the buyer would evaluate them as completely different businesses, it probably doesn't. The peer group should reflect real competitive and operating comparability, not just sector label."

Section 3.3 — Building the Numbers: Step by Step
With the peer group selected, the analyst builds out the financial data. Each step requires specific attention to data quality.
Step 1: Pull Market Data Download share prices, shares outstanding (diluted), and debt and cash figures from the most recent filings (10-K, 10-Q) and financial databases (Bloomberg, FactSet, Capital IQ). Always date-stamp market data — multiples from stale share prices are meaningless in a live deal.
Step 2: Compute Enterprise Value Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Total Debt + Preferred Equity + Minority Interest − Cash Note: "Total debt" means all interest-bearing debt obligations. Accounts payable are not debt. Operating lease liabilities may or may not be included depending on the analysis convention — but the treatment must be consistent across all peers.
Step 3: Calendarize Companies use different fiscal year-ends. If the subject company has a December fiscal year-end, comparing it to a peer with a September fiscal year-end on reported annual figures introduces a timing mismatch. Calendarization adjusts each company's financial data to the same trailing twelve-month period so that LTM figures are truly comparable.
l-formula block:
LTM CALENDARIZATION — THE STANDARD METHOD
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
LTM Metric = Full Fiscal Year + Most Recent Interim Period − Prior Year Same Period

Example (EBITDA):
  FY EBITDA (Dec 2024 year-end):          $180M
  Q1 2025 (Jan–Mar 2025) EBITDA:          $52M
  Q1 2024 (Jan–Mar 2024) EBITDA:         ($47M)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  LTM EBITDA (as of March 2025):          $185M

This adjustment ensures all LTM figures in the table reflect the same trailing
twelve-month period — essential for apples-to-apples comparison.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Step 4: Scrub for Non-Recurring Items Raw reported EBITDA includes noise: restructuring charges, one-time litigation settlements, gain/loss on asset sales, acquisition-related costs. These items distort the comparison. A company that recorded $50M of restructuring charges in the year looks worse on a raw EBITDA basis than a peer that did not — even if their underlying operations are identical.
Scrubbing means reviewing the earnings release and footnotes to identify non-recurring items and add them back (for charges) or subtract them (for gains) to arrive at a normalized, recurring EBITDA figure. Every adjustment must be documented and defensible. Overly aggressive scrubbing — adding back everything management calls "non-recurring" including items that clearly recur — overstates EBITDA and is an analytical error.
Step 5: Pull NTM Consensus Forward estimates come from consensus databases (Bloomberg, FactSet, Capital IQ). Use the median consensus estimate, not a single analyst's projection. Note the number of estimates — a forward NTM EBITDA based on one analyst estimate should be treated with more skepticism than one based on 15 analysts.
Step 6: Compute Multiples and Summary Statistics With EV and financial metrics in place, multiples are simple arithmetic. The analytical work is in the preceding five steps. The table should include 25th percentile, median, mean, and 75th percentile for each multiple. Outliers — companies with extreme multiples that distort the mean — should be flagged in the notes.

LESSON 4 — PRECEDENT TRANSACTIONS: HOW M&A DEALS CHANGE THE MULTIPLES
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: ib_m4_l4
Difficulty: Advanced
Estimated Time: 11 minutes
Key Terms Introduced: Precedent Transactions, Control Premium, Strategic Buyer, Financial Buyer, LTM at Announcement

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"When LinkedIn was acquired by Microsoft in 2016 for $26.2 billion, the deal implied an EV/Revenue multiple of approximately 7.5x. LinkedIn's public comps — companies like Facebook and Twitter — were trading at 4–5x revenue at the time. The 50%+ premium was not irrational. It was the price of control, the strategic value of LinkedIn's network to Microsoft's enterprise ambitions, and the competitive dynamics of the acquisition process. That gap between trading multiple and transaction multiple is the subject of this lesson."

Lesson Terms
Term
Definition
Precedent Transactions
Relative valuation method using multiples implied by completed M&A deals.
Control Premium
Incremental value paid above standalone trading price to acquire a controlling interest. Typically 20–40%.
Strategic Buyer
A company in the same or adjacent industry acquiring for operational or competitive benefit.
Financial Buyer
A private equity or other financial sponsor acquiring primarily for return on invested capital.
LTM at Announcement
The LTM financial figures of the target company at the time the deal was publicly announced. The standard reference point for transaction multiples.


Section 4.1 — What Precedent Transactions Tell You That Comps Cannot
A comps table tells you what the market pays for minority stakes in publicly traded companies today. A precedent transactions table tells you what acquirers have paid to take control of entire businesses in negotiated transactions.
The difference matters for three reasons:
Control premium. When acquiring a company outright, a buyer must compensate existing shareholders for the loss of the option to sell at a future, potentially higher price — and for the liquidity discount of exiting a public position. The control premium also reflects the synergies the buyer expects to realize. Acquirers typically pay 20–40% above the unaffected trading price in public company M&A.
Strategic value. A company purchased by a strategic acquirer may be worth more to that specific buyer than it would be as a standalone entity. Cost synergies (eliminating duplicate functions), revenue synergies (cross-selling into each other's customer bases), and market consolidation value all justify paying above the standalone public market price.
Transaction certainty premium. In a competitive auction, multiple bidders drive up the price. The winning bid often reflects not just what one buyer thinks the business is worth, but what they needed to pay to win against a competitive process. This dynamic systematically produces higher multiples than public market trading values.
l-callout block: Label: WHY TRANSACTION MULTIPLES ARE ALMOST ALWAYS HIGHER THAN TRADING MULTIPLES
"In every pitch book I've ever seen, the precedent transactions range sits above the comps range in the football field chart. That's not a coincidence. Acquiring control of a business requires paying for three things the comps table doesn't include: a control premium, expected synergies, and competitive tension in the deal process. When a banker presents transaction comps to a board weighing a sale, those higher multiples are the evidence that selling may crystallize value above the current stock price. Understanding that spread is the core of M&A advisory work."

Section 4.3 — Selecting Relevant Precedents
Precedent transactions are harder to select than comps because the universe is smaller and the transactions are historical. Key selection criteria:
Business similarity: Same as comps — the target must operate a comparable business.
Time period: More recent transactions are more relevant — market conditions, interest rates, and deal activity levels change over time. Transactions older than five years may be less applicable, though in thin transaction histories you may need to include older deals and explicitly flag the vintage.
Deal type: Strategic versus financial buyer transactions should be presented separately or clearly labeled. Financial sponsor (PE) deals are structured around debt capacity and return thresholds — they systematically produce lower multiples than strategic deals in the same sector. Mixing them without labeling distorts the distribution.
Deal status: Completed transactions are preferable to withdrawn or failed deals. A failed deal represents a price the market ultimately rejected — its multiples are interesting context but should not be weighted equally with completed transactions.

A sophisticated M&A analysis always shows both comps and precedent transactions, with an explicit discussion of the spread between them.

LESSON 5 — THE FOOTBALL FIELD CHART: COMMUNICATING A VALUATION RANGE
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: ib_m4_l5
Difficulty: Advanced
Estimated Time: 8 minutes
Key Terms Introduced: Football Field Chart, Implied Equity Value, Valuation Range, Bridge

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"An MD once told me: 'The football field is the most important slide in the pitch book. Not because it's the most rigorous analysis — it isn't. It's because it's the only slide where the board can see in thirty seconds whether the deal makes sense. If the current stock price is at the bottom of every range on the football field, the company looks like it should be sold. If it's at the top, the management team can defend its position. The chart is the conclusion of everything you've built.'"

Lesson Terms
Term
Definition
Football Field Chart
A horizontal bar chart summarizing valuation ranges from multiple methodologies. The standard IB deliverable for communicating a valuation conclusion.
Implied Equity Value
Valuation conclusion expressed per share, derived by applying selected multiples to the subject company's metrics and bridging from EV to equity value.
EV to Equity Bridge
Calculation converting enterprise value to equity value: EV − Net Debt − Preferred − Minority Interest = Equity Value; divided by diluted shares to get per-share value.


Section 5.1 — What the Football Field Is and What It Does
The football field chart gets its name from its visual similarity to a gridiron — a series of horizontal bars stretching across a yardage scale. Each bar represents one valuation methodology: DCF, public comps, precedent transactions, 52-week trading range, analyst price targets, and any other approach deployed in the analysis.
The bars do not show point estimates. They show ranges — the low end of the plausible valuation from each methodology to the high end. The current trading price (or the proposed deal price in an M&A context) is shown as a vertical line cutting across all the bars. The message of the chart is delivered visually: does the proposed price sit in the middle of the ranges? Is it at the high end of every methodology (suggesting the deal may be fully valued or expensive)? Is it below every range (suggesting the company is cheap or a deal is favorable to the buyer)?
The football field is a synthesis document. It does not add new analysis. It takes the outputs from four to six separate analyses built earlier in the pitch book and presents them on a single page so the key decision-makers — the board, the CEO, or the prospective buyer — can absorb the full valuation picture in seconds.

Section 5.2 — The EV to Equity Value Bridge
Before a football field can be presented in per-share terms, the analyst must convert each methodology's EV output into an implied equity value per share. This bridge is the same for every methodology.
l-formula block:
EV TO EQUITY VALUE PER SHARE — THE STANDARD BRIDGE

Enterprise Value (from valuation methodology)       $X,XXX M
  − Total Debt                                       (XXX M)
  − Preferred Stock (if any)                          (XX M)
  − Minority Interest (if any)                        (XX M)
  + Cash and Cash Equivalents                         +XXX M
                                                    ──────────
= Equity Value                                      $X,XXX M

Equity Value Per Share = Equity Value / Diluted Shares Outstanding

Diluted shares = basic shares + options + warrants + RSUs + convertible
                 securities (using treasury stock method)

using the same balance sheet as of the analysis date.
Consistency is essential — the same debt and cash figures
are used across all methodologies.
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 5.3 — Building the Football Field: Row by Row
A complete football field for an investment banking pitch typically includes five to seven rows. Each row represents one methodology:
Row 1: 52-Week Trading Range The lowest and highest closing prices of the subject company's stock over the past 52 weeks. This is not a fundamental valuation — it is a market data reference that anchors the other ranges to actual observed trading prices. Boards and CEOs immediately understand this row because they follow their stock price.
Row 2: Analyst Price Targets The range of 12-month price targets from sell-side analysts covering the stock. Anchors the football field to current Wall Street consensus. If the proposed price is significantly above the highest analyst target, it signals the deal is being priced ahead of even the most optimistic external view.
Row 3: Public Comps (EV/EBITDA) The range implied by applying the 25th to 75th percentile of the peer group's EV/EBITDA multiple to the subject company's NTM EBITDA, then bridging to equity value. Shows where the market currently prices comparable businesses.
Row 4: Public Comps (EV/Revenue) Used alongside EV/EBITDA when the industry is valued on both — or when the subject company's margins differ enough from the peer median to make the revenue multiple a useful cross-check.
Row 5: Precedent Transactions The range implied by applying the 25th to 75th percentile of transaction multiples to the subject company's LTM metrics. Includes the control premium inherent in M&A deals. Typically sits above the comps rows.
Row 6: DCF The range produced by the discounted cash flow analysis, typically spanning from a bear case (lower growth, higher WACC) to a bull case (higher growth, lower WACC). Represents intrinsic value independent of market pricing.
Row 7: LBO Floor (for sell-side M&A) In a sale process, the analyst often includes an LBO analysis showing the minimum price a financial sponsor could pay and still achieve a target return (typically 20–25% IRR). This is the floor of what a PE buyer can rationally offer.

Section 5.4 — Reading and Interpreting the Chart
The football field is analytical, not merely decorative. An experienced banker reads the chart for four things:
1. Convergence: Do the ranges from multiple methodologies overlap significantly? Convergence increases confidence that the valuation is grounded. Divergence signals model sensitivity or structural differences between methodologies that need to be investigated and explained.
2. Position of the current stock price: Is the stock in the middle of the ranges (fairly valued per this analysis), below all ranges (potentially undervalued), or at the top (fully valued or expensive)? This positioning is the most important communication the chart delivers to a board weighing strategic alternatives.
3. Relative positions of comps vs. transactions: The gap between the comps range and the precedent transaction range represents the estimated control premium available in a sale process. A large gap argues for exploring a sale; a small gap suggests the public markets are already pricing near-acquisition value.
4. Width of the DCF range: If the DCF range is very wide, the company's value is highly sensitive to assumptions — a signal of uncertainty that should be discussed explicitly. If it is narrow, the business has predictable economics.
l-callout block: Label: WHAT AN MD READS IN THE FOOTBALL FIELD
"The first thing I look at when I see a football field is whether the current trading price is above or below the comps range. If it's below, the stock appears cheap — and I want to know why. Is the market pricing in a business deterioration our analysis isn't capturing, or is it genuine undervaluation? If the stock is above the comps range but below the transactions range, there's a classic privatization premium story — a buyer can pay above market and still justify it on transaction comps. The chart tells the narrative; the rest of the pitch book tells the evidence."

Section 5.5 — Common Mistakes in Constructing Football Fields
l-list:
Inconsistent bridge across methodologies: Using different balance sheet dates or different share counts for different rows introduces inconsistency that makes the per-share values incomparable. Use the same bridge inputs for every methodology.


Selecting ranges without analytical justification: The low and high of each range should correspond to a specific multiple (e.g., 25th percentile comps multiple as the low, 75th percentile as the high) or a specific scenario (bear case vs. bull case in DCF). Arbitrary range selection is both analytically indefensible and visible to experienced reviewers.


Excluding methodologies that produce inconvenient ranges: The football field should include all methodologies run. Omitting the DCF because it produces a lower value than comps, or excluding a relevant precedent transaction because it would widen the range, is selective disclosure. An MD reviewing the analysis will notice.


Treating the chart as the answer rather than the synthesis: The football field communicates the output of the analysis. It does not replace the peer group work, the transaction selection, or the DCF assumptions. A beautiful football field built on a weak comps table or an untested DCF is a polished presentation of flawed work.

Step 2 — Build the Market Data Block For each peer: pull current share price, diluted shares outstanding (from most recent 10-K or 10-Q), total debt (interest-bearing obligations), cash and equivalents. Compute market cap and enterprise value. Date-stamp all market data.
Step 3 — Compute LTM Financials For each peer: calendarize to the same trailing twelve-month period. Use the LTM formula (Full FY + Most Recent Quarter − Prior Year Same Quarter) to ensure temporal alignment. Flag any peers with non-December fiscal year-ends.
Step 4 — Scrub for Non-Recurring Items Review the most recent earnings releases for each peer. Add back one-time restructuring charges, acquisition-related costs, and litigation settlements that are genuinely non-recurring. Document each adjustment in the notes tab.
Step 5 — Pull NTM Consensus From Bloomberg, FactSet, or Capital IQ: pull NTM Revenue, EBITDA, and EPS consensus estimates for each peer. Note the number of analysts contributing.
Step 6 — Compute Multiples and Summary Statistics Compute EV/LTM Revenue, EV/NTM Revenue, EV/LTM EBITDA, EV/NTM EBITDA, LTM P/E, and NTM P/E for each peer. Calculate 25th percentile, median, mean, and 75th percentile.
Step 7 — Apply to WTS and Compute Implied Value Range Apply the peer median EV/NTM EBITDA and EV/NTM Revenue multiples to WTS's NTM estimates. Bridge from implied EV to equity value per share using WTS's balance sheet.

Part 2: Build the Precedent Transactions Table
Instructions
Identify 6–8 completed M&A transactions in the flow control, water infrastructure, or adjacent industrial equipment space from the past 5–7 years. For each transaction:
Record the announcement date, target, acquirer, buyer type (strategic vs. PE)
Determine the transaction enterprise value (deal equity consideration + assumed debt)
Pull LTM financials of the target as of the announcement date
Compute EV/LTM Revenue, EV/LTM EBITDA, and premium to unaffected share price
Note any outlier transactions and explain why they should be included or excluded
Apply the transaction median and 25th/75th percentile multiples to WTS LTM figures to derive a precedent transaction implied value range.

Part 3: Build the Football Field Chart
Instructions
Construct the football field with the following rows:
52-Week Trading Range (direct market data — no computation needed)
Analyst Price Targets (use current Bloomberg consensus targets — range from low to high)

Public Comps — EV/NTM Revenue (25th–75th percentile applied to WTS)

For each row: show a horizontal bar spanning the implied per-share range. Mark the current WTS trading price ($150/share) as a vertical dashed line. Label each bar with the methodology name and implied range.

Part 4: Written Analysis
Complete your deliverable with a 1-page written analysis addressing:
1. Peer group rationale: Why did you include each company? Why did you exclude any candidates you considered?
2. Multiple selection and application: Which multiple did you weight most heavily for WTS and why? Are there structural differences between WTS and the peer median that justify a discount or premium?
3. Football field interpretation: Where does the current trading price sit relative to the ranges? What does the spread between comps and transaction multiples suggest about the control premium available in a potential sale?
4. Investment banking recommendation: Based solely on the valuation analysis (not strategic factors), does the current market price appear to reflect, discount, or exceed WTS's fundamental value? If WTS were sold today at the transaction comps median, would that represent a good outcome for shareholders?

APPENDIX B — COMMON MISTAKES IN COMPS AND TRANSACTIONS ANALYSIS
1. Mixing Enterprise Value with Equity Value Metrics The single most frequent junior analyst error. EV/EBITDA and EV/Revenue are enterprise value multiples — the numerator includes debt. P/E and P/B are equity multiples — the numerator is market cap only. Using equity value in the numerator against EBITDA in the denominator is an illegal pairing that produces a nonsense number. Cross-check every multiple: if the numerator reflects all capital providers, the denominator must too.
2. Forgetting to Dilute for Options and Warrants Diluted shares are the correct share count for computing market capitalization in a comps table. Basic shares understates market cap by ignoring the dilutive securities that would convert into shares at current prices. The treasury stock method should be applied to options and warrants: assume proceeds from option exercise are used to repurchase shares at the current market price.
3. Comparing LTM Multiples to NTM Multiples Across Peers If half the peers are valued on LTM figures and the other half on NTM (because different fiscal year reporting creates LTM periods that are effectively forward for some companies), the comparison is temporally inconsistent. Calendarize all companies to the same period.
4. Including Outliers Without Flagging Them Every peer group has at least one company that trades at a significant premium or discount to the group due to company-specific circumstances: a company with a pending M&A catalyst, a company that recently issued a profit warning, or a company with a unique business mix. Include them but flag them clearly. Present ranges both including and excluding material outliers. An MD reviewing a comps table immediately sees the outlier — if the analysis doesn't address it, the analyst looks like they didn't see it.
5. Using Management's "Adjusted" EBITDA Without Scrutiny Companies publish non-GAAP "adjusted EBITDA" in their earnings releases. These adjustments almost always make the company look better than GAAP would. Accepting management's adjusted figures wholesale without independent review is an analytical failure. Review each adjustment: is it genuinely non-recurring? Does the same adjustment apply consistently across all peers? Apply consistent scrubbing standards, not company- specific ones.
6. Stale Market Data A comps table built on prices from two weeks ago is wrong. Market caps and EV change every day with the stock price. In a live deal, market data is refreshed daily. In an academic context, date-stamp the analysis and note the market data date. Never present a comps table without a clear "as of" date on the market data.
