MODULE GLOSSARY — DCF TERMS
These 20 terms populate the sidebar glossary for this module and appear as inline term cards at first introduction.
Term
Definition
Beta (β)
Measure of a stock's sensitivity to market movements. β > 1 means more volatile than the market; β < 1 means less.
Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)
Formula that derives the required return on equity: Rf + β × (Rm − Rf).
Capital Expenditures (CapEx)
Cash spent acquiring or maintaining fixed assets. Reduces free cash flow.
Cost of Debt (Kd)
The effective interest rate a company pays on its borrowings, adjusted for tax shield.
Cost of Equity (Ke)
The return required by equity investors, typically derived via CAPM.
Discount Rate
The rate used to convert future cash flows into present value. Reflects risk and time cost.
Enterprise Value (EV)
Total value of the firm to all capital providers: equity value + net debt.
Equity Value
The value attributable solely to shareholders: EV minus net debt.
Exit Multiple
A valuation multiple applied to a terminal-year metric to estimate terminal value.
Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)
NOPAT + D&A − CapEx − Change in Working Capital. Cash available to all capital providers.
Gordon Growth Model
Terminal value formula: FCF × (1+g) / (WACC − g). Assumes perpetual growth at rate g.
Intrinsic Value
The present value of all expected future cash flows. Independent of market prices.
Net Operating Profit After Tax (NOPAT)
EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate). After-tax operating profit before financing effects.
Risk-Free Rate (Rf)
The return on a theoretically riskless investment, typically the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield.
Sensitivity Analysis
A table showing how the model's output changes as key assumptions vary.
Tax Shield
The tax reduction created by the deductibility of interest expense.
Terminal Value (TV)
The estimated value of all cash flows beyond the explicit forecast period.
Unlevered Beta
Beta with the effect of capital structure removed. Used to re-lever for comparable analysis.
WACC
Weighted Average Cost of Capital. The blended required return across all capital providers.
Working Capital (Net)
Operating current assets minus operating current liabilities. Changes drive cash flow.


LESSON 1 — WHY DCF IS THE FOUNDATION OF ALL VALUATION
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: val_m3_l1
Difficulty: Advanced
Estimated Time: 20 minutes
Prerequisites: Module 1 (Financial Statements), Module 2 (Ratio Analysis)
Feeds Into: Lessons 2–7; Module 4 (Comps), Course 3 (M&A)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"In 2018, a hedge fund analyst presented a DCF showing that Amazon's stock was worth $2,400 per share — when it was trading at $1,900. Her MD asked one question: 'What's your WACC?' She said 8.5%. He said, 'Run it at 10. What do you get?' She ran it. The answer was $1,600. Her entire $500 premium to the market disappeared because of a 150-basis-point change in one input. That is the DCF. It is the most powerful and most fragile tool in finance. Understanding how to build it — and where it breaks — is the first thing every analyst learns and the last thing they stop thinking about."

Section 1.1 — What Intrinsic Value Actually Means
Situation
A stock trades at $45. A comparable company in the same sector was acquired last month at a 12x EBITDA multiple — implying this stock is worth $62. A sell-side analyst has a $38 price target. A momentum quant model says $50. Who is right?
The DCF framework answers this question from first principles: what is this company actually worth, independent of what anyone is willing to pay for it today?
Teaching Content
l-body:
Intrinsic value is the present value of all future cash flows that a business is expected to generate over its remaining life. It is not a market price. It is not a consensus. It is not a multiple of earnings. It is what the business is worth — in the mathematical sense — if you could own it outright and collect every future cash flow it will ever produce, discounted back to today at a rate that reflects the risk of receiving those cash flows.
The DCF is the mechanical implementation of this idea. It forces the analyst to be explicit about three things that every other valuation method leaves implicit: the growth of future cash flows, the risk embedded in those cash flows, and when the cash flows actually arrive. Multiples-based valuation — EV/EBITDA comps, precedent transaction analysis — obscures all three. When a company trades at 12x EBITDA and you benchmark it against peers trading at 14x, you are not analyzing value. You are comparing opinions. The DCF requires you to form your own.
l-callout block: Label: ANALYST MINDSET
"Every time I build a comps table, I'm asking: what does the market think? Every time I build a DCF, I'm asking: what do I think? Those two questions should produce different answers. If your DCF spits out exactly where the stock is trading, you haven't done analysis — you've reverse-engineered the current price and dressed it up as a model. The value of the DCF is in the disagreement. Where your assumptions diverge from what the market is implying is exactly where the investment thesis lives."

Section 1.2 — The Five Inputs That Drive Everything
l-body:
The DCF has exactly five moving parts. Every assumption in a 2,000-row financial model ultimately flows into one of these five categories:
l-formula block:
THE DCF FRAMEWORK — FIVE COMPONENTS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
1. EXPLICIT FORECAST PERIOD
   → Typically 5–10 years of projected Free Cash Flows
   → Long enough to capture the strategic investment horizon
   → Short enough that projections remain defensible

2. FREE CASH FLOW TO THE FIRM (FCFF) — each forecast year
   → The numerator: what the business actually generates
   → NOPAT + D&A − CapEx − ΔNWC

3. DISCOUNT RATE (WACC)
   → The denominator: what rate of return do capital providers demand?
   → Blended cost across equity and debt, weighted by capital structure

4. TERMINAL VALUE
   → Value attributed to all cash flows beyond the forecast period
   → Typically 60–80% of total DCF value — the most important and
     most disputed component of any model

5. NET DEBT & SHARES OUTSTANDING
   → Bridge from Enterprise Value → Equity Value → Price per share
   → EV − Net Debt = Equity Value ÷ Diluted Shares = Intrinsic Price
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

l-body:
Each of these five components introduces analyst judgment. The forecast period requires you to decide how long the business will be in its high-growth phase. The FCF projection requires a view on revenues, margins, and capital intensity. The WACC requires an opinion on risk. The terminal value requires a view on the company's long-term economics. This is why the DCF is not a machine that produces right answers — it is a framework that forces rigorous, explicit assumptions and shows you the consequences of being wrong.

Section 1.3 — Why Every Valuation Method Traces Back to DCF
l-body:
Every shortcut valuation method is a DCF in disguise. When you value a company at 15x EBITDA, you are implicitly making DCF assumptions — you have embedded a growth rate, a margin assumption, a capital intensity assumption, and a discount rate into that single multiple. You just can't see them. The DCF makes them visible.
l-formula block:
MULTIPLES AS IMPLICIT DCFS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
EV/EBITDA Multiple of 12x:
  → Implicitly assumes: ~5% growth, ~25% CapEx/EBITDA conversion,
    ~8% WACC, stable margins
  → Change any of these hidden assumptions: the "right" multiple shifts

P/E Multiple of 20x:
  → Implies: earnings grow at ~5% per year indefinitely at ~10% cost of equity
  → At 15% growth assumed: the "correct" P/E rises to ~30–35x

The DCF analyst's advantage:
  → Sees what assumptions drive a comparable's multiple
  → Can identify when the market is paying for growth that won't materialize
  → Or paying too little for growth the market hasn't priced
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Lesson 1 Quiz
quiz-wrap
Question 1 — Comprehension: A company trades at a market cap of $4.2B. Your DCF, built with explicit revenue, margin, and WACC assumptions, produces an intrinsic value of $3.5B. What is the most analytically productive interpretation?
A) The market is wrong and the stock should be shorted B) Your model is wrong and should be revised to match the market price C) Your assumptions differ from the market's implied assumptions; identify which assumptions drive the gap and assess whether your view is defensible D) The DCF and the market price are measuring different things and cannot be compared
Answer: C | Explanation: A gap between a DCF output and market price is the beginning of analysis, not its conclusion. The correct response is to reverse-engineer what assumptions would produce the current market price (implied growth rate, implied WACC) and compare those to your own. If your growth rate is 6% and the market's implied growth is 9%, you have a specific, debatable disagreement — that is where the investment thesis lives. Neither "the market is wrong" nor "my model is wrong" is a complete answer without understanding which specific assumptions diverge and why.

Question 2 — Application: An analyst builds a DCF and gets exactly the current trading price. A senior banker says: "Start over." Why?
A) The analyst probably made a mathematical error B) A DCF that matches the current price has no predictive value — it only proves the model can reproduce the market price C) The correct DCF output should always show the stock as undervalued D) The discount rate is probably too high
Answer: B | Explanation: A DCF that outputs the current trading price is a tautology — it tells you the market is right, which you already knew from the stock screen. The purpose of a DCF is independent valuation: arriving at a defensible view of intrinsic value through explicit assumptions about future cash flows. If those assumptions happen to produce the market price, the model provides no new information. The analyst should form their own view on each assumption and allow the model to produce whatever value follows from those assumptions — whether above, below, or at the market price.

LESSON 2 — PROJECTING REVENUE: TOP-DOWN VS. BOTTOM-UP FORECASTING
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: val_m3_l2
Difficulty: Advanced
Estimated Time: 25 minutes
Prerequisites: Lesson 1; Module 1 Section 1.2 (Revenue Quality)
Feeds Into: Lesson 3 (Margins, CapEx), Lesson 4 (FCFF)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"A sell-side analyst covering a regional hospital network was forecasting 12% revenue growth for the next three years because the company's management guided to 12%. His MD walked over and asked: 'What's the total addressable market? What's their current market share? What would their share have to reach for that revenue to be real?' He couldn't answer. His forecast wasn't a forecast — it was a transcription of management guidance. At Goldman, you are expected to know the difference. This lesson teaches you how to build a revenue projection that you can actually defend."

Section 2.1 — Two Philosophies, One Answer
Teaching Content
l-body:
Revenue is the single most consequential line in a DCF model. An error in the WACC by 50 basis points matters. An error in the long-term revenue growth rate by 200 basis points matters more. The projection methodology you choose determines whether your revenue forecast is grounded in observable reality or built on compounded hope.
Analysts use two primary methodologies, often in combination:
l-formula block:
REVENUE FORECASTING METHODOLOGIES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
TOP-DOWN APPROACH
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Market Size (TAM)
  × Market Share (current + projected gain)
  = Company Revenue

  Logic: Start with the market the company operates in.
  Estimate how much of that market the company will capture.
  Revenue is a residual of market dynamics.

  Best for: High-growth companies, new markets, competitive dynamics
  analyses, companies with <30% current market penetration
  Risk: TAM definitions are often inflated; market share gain
        assumptions can be heroically optimistic

BOTTOM-UP APPROACH
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Volume Drivers × Price Assumptions = Revenue

  Example — Retail Chain:
    Number of Stores × Revenue per Store = Total Revenue
    Stores = Current count + New openings − Closures
    Rev/Store = Comp store growth (volume + price)

  Example — SaaS:
    Customers × Average Revenue per Customer (ARPU) = ARR
    New Customers = Sales capacity × Win rate
    Churn = Gross churn − Expansion

  Logic: Build revenue from unit-level drivers that are
  observable, benchmarkable, and testable against history.
  Best for: Established businesses with identifiable revenue units
  Risk: Requires granular data; can miss step-change dynamics
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 2.2 — The Top-Down Framework
l-body:
Top-down forecasting begins with market structure. Before a single revenue number is typed into a model, the analyst establishes three things: how large is the market, how fast is it growing, and what is the company's current and defensible share of that market.
Constructing the TAM:
Not all TAM estimates are created equal. Management teams in investor presentations define their TAM as broadly as possible to make the opportunity sound large. An analyst's job is to define the realistic serviceable market — the portion the company can actually reach with its current products, channels, and geography.
l-example block: Label: TOP-DOWN BUILD — HEALTHCARE ANALYTICS SOFTWARE
Company: DataMed Analytics (mid-cap, $380M revenue) Business: Sells data analytics software to U.S. hospitals and health systems
Step 1 — Define the realistic serviceable market: Total U.S. hospital IT spend: $22B annually Addressable analytics software subset: $6.4B DataMed's segments served: $3.8B (mid/large hospitals only) → Realistic TAM: $3.8B
Step 2 — Current market share: DataMed Revenue: $380M / $3.8B TAM = 10.0% share
Step 3 — Market growth rate: Hospital IT spend historically: +5–7% per year Analytics software premium (AI/workflow demand): +2–3% on top → Market growth assumption: 7.5% annually
Step 4 — Share assumption: Management claims "best-in-class" product; last 3 years: share held flat Conservative analyst view: share stable at 10.0% Base case revenue growth = Market growth = 7.5% per year
Year 1 forecast: $380M × 1.075 = $408.5M ✓

Section 2.3 — The Bottom-Up Framework
l-body:
Bottom-up forecasting builds revenue from component drivers. The discipline forces the analyst to identify what actually creates revenue at the unit level — and whether those unit drivers are growing, stable, or deteriorating.
l-formula block:
BOTTOM-UP REVENUE DRIVER STRUCTURE
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
UNIT DRIVER EXAMPLES BY BUSINESS TYPE:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SaaS Company:
  Beginning ARR
  + New ARR (Sales capacity × Quota × Win rate)
  − Churned ARR (Gross churn % × Beginning ARR)
  + Expansion ARR (Upsell % × Installed base)
  = Ending ARR
  → Revenue ≈ Average ARR for period (accounting for linearity)

Retail Chain:
  Prior store count
  + Net new stores (openings − closures)
  × Revenue per store
  = Total Revenue
  Where Revenue/store = Prior period × (1 + SSS growth)
  SSS = Same-Store Sales growth (volume + price components)

Specialty Manufacturer:
  Units shipped × Average Selling Price (ASP)
  Units: Capacity utilization × Production capacity
  ASP: Prior ASP × (1 + pricing power assumption)
  → Separate pricing from volume — they have different risk profiles

Industrial Services Company:
  Backlog at period start
  + New contract wins
  − Revenue recognized
  = Backlog at period end
  Revenue from backlog burn rate + spot work
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

l-callout block: Label: THE MODELING DISCIPLINE
"Never enter a growth rate without knowing what it implies in absolute dollars. A 7.5% growth assumption on a $380M business means adding $28M of new revenue per year. Ask yourself: is that operationally feasible? Does that require hiring? Does it imply market share gains? Always translate one into the other before locking the model."

Lesson 2 Quiz
quiz-wrap
Question 1 — Application: An analyst uses a bottom-up build for a SaaS company and projects 800 new customers in Year 1, based on current sales team capacity. The company's sales team is 40 reps with an average quota of $800K ARR. Historically, reps achieve 75% of quota. What is the implied new ARR from this sales capacity?
A) $32M B) $24M C) $19.2M D) $20M
Answer: B | Explanation: 40 reps × $800K quota = $32M of quota capacity. At 75% attainment, new ARR = $32M × 75% = $24M. The bottom-up discipline is that this $24M has a specific, observable driver — headcount and historical quota attainment — rather than an arbitrary percentage growth assumption. The analyst can test this assumption against the company's hiring plan and disclosed quota attainment rates.

Question 2 — Judgment: A company's management guidance projects 15% revenue growth for the next 3 years. The analyst's top-down model, using realistic TAM growth and stable market share, produces 8% growth. What should the analyst do?
A) Use management guidance — they have better information B) Use the 8% figure — the DCF must be independent of management C) Investigate the source of the 6-7% gap: is management projecting share gains, new products, or M&A? Build explicit assumptions for each and decide whether they are defensible D) Average the two figures at ~11.5% to reduce model risk
Answer: C | Explanation: The correct response is investigation, not resolution by default. Management guidance typically assumes market share gains, new product launches, or pricing actions that the top-down model does not include. The analyst's job is to identify every source of that 6–7% gap, evaluate each independently, and decide which assumptions are credible. If management's share gain thesis is defensible, include it. If it relies on product launches with uncertain timelines or market share gains against entrenched competitors, discount it. The discipline is to disaggregate the gap into specific, testable assumptions — not to take sides blindly.

LESSON 3 — PROJECTING MARGINS, CAPEX, AND WORKING CAPITAL
Lesson Metadata

Feeds Into: Lesson 4 (FCFF build)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"A first-year analyst builds a DCF for a consumer products company. Revenue is well-researched. But he carries the current EBITDA margin forward flat for five years. His associate asks: 'Why is the margin flat? This company just announced a $200M distribution center investment. What happens to margins while it's being built? What happens after?' The analyst hadn't thought about it. He'd projected revenue with precision and projected the margin with a copy-paste. The DCF was incomplete. The second analyst — the one who got the good review — had projected margins, CapEx, and working capital on separate schedules with specific drivers for each. This lesson teaches you to build those schedules."

Section 3.1 — Projecting EBITDA Margins
l-body:
Margins are not constants. They are the output of a cost structure interacting with a revenue volume. Three distinct forces determine whether EBITDA margins expand, contract, or remain stable:
l-list:
Operating leverage: When a business has significant fixed costs, margins expand as revenue grows because fixed costs are spread over more revenue dollars. A company with $100M of fixed costs and $400M revenue has a 25% cost ratio from those fixed costs. At $600M revenue, the same $100M costs become a 16.7% ratio — a 830-basis-point margin expansion without any cost reduction. This is operating leverage, and it is the primary driver of margin expansion in scaled businesses.


Pricing power vs. input cost dynamics: Even with flat fixed-cost leverage, margins can expand if a company raises prices faster than its input costs rise, or contract if input costs (raw materials, wages, freight) outpace pricing. Industrial companies and consumer goods companies spend significant time in models on this dynamic — particularly in inflationary environments.


Investment cycle effects: Companies undergoing capacity expansion, geographic expansion, or technology platform build-outs typically experience transient margin compression as they absorb the operating cost of new capacity before it generates full revenue. Modeling this correctly requires understanding the timing of the investment thesis, not just the steady-state economics.


l-formula block:
MARGIN PROJECTION FRAMEWORK
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
GROSS MARGIN DRIVERS:
  → Product mix shift (higher/lower margin segments growing faster)
  → Input cost trends (materials, labor, freight)
  → Pricing power (ability to raise prices relative to competitors)
  → Scale effects in production (volume purchasing, yield improvement)

EBITDA MARGIN DRIVERS (beyond gross margin):
  → SG&A leverage ratio: SG&A / Revenue — should decline in scale businesses
  → R&D investment cycle: increasing in early product phase, stabilizing later
  → Headcount efficiency: Revenue per employee (key metric in services/SaaS)

EBITDA MARGIN PROJECTION STRUCTURE:
  Year 0 (Actual):  24.5% EBITDA margin
  Year 1–2:         25.0–25.8%  → early operating leverage from revenue growth
  Year 3–4:         26.5–27.0%  → full-run leverage + SG&A efficiency
  Year 5 (terminal): 27.5%      → normalized long-run margin
  Key test: Is this margin trajectory consistent with peer benchmarks?
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 3.2 — Projecting Capital Expenditures
l-body:
CapEx is among the most consequential and most frequently oversimplified inputs in a DCF model. It directly reduces free cash flow — every dollar of CapEx is a dollar the business cannot distribute to capital providers. Yet analysts routinely project CapEx as a flat percentage of revenue without understanding what is driving it.
l-formula block:
THE TWO-COMPONENT CAPEX FRAMEWORK
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
MAINTENANCE CAPEX
  → Spending required to sustain existing productive capacity
  → Does not generate incremental revenue; prevents revenue decline
  → Proxy: often estimated as % of PP&E or replacement of D&A
  → Rule of thumb: Maintenance CapEx ≈ Depreciation in steady state
  → If CapEx < D&A for sustained periods: business is under-investing
    (asset base is shrinking in real terms — flag for analysts)

GROWTH CAPEX
  → Spending that generates incremental future revenue or capacity
  → Must be linked to the revenue growth assumption
  → If revenue grows 7.5%/year but CapEx drops to maintenance levels:
    the model is inconsistent — growth requires investment
  → Return on Growth CapEx = Incremental Revenue / Growth CapEx
    (compare to WACC to assess whether growth is value-creating)

BUILDING THE CAPEX SCHEDULE:
  Year 1–2: Elevated CapEx (expansion phase):    8.5% of Revenue
  Year 3–4: Moderation as expansion completes:   7.0% of Revenue
  Year 5+:  Normalized maintenance-heavy ratio:  5.5% of Revenue

Test: Is this consistent with PP&E growth on the balance sheet?
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 3.3 — Projecting Working Capital
l-body:
Working capital changes consume or release cash in ways that the income statement completely obscures. A business growing revenue rapidly will generally consume working capital — it ships more product, has more receivables outstanding, and must hold more inventory before those receivables are collected. A business in a slow-growth or declining phase may release working capital as it collects on prior sales and draws down inventory — generating cash without generating profits.
l-formula block:
NET WORKING CAPITAL IN A DCF — WHAT TO PROJECT
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Operating NWC = AR + Inventory + Prepaid Expenses
              − AP − Accrued Liabilities − Deferred Revenue

Key principle: Project each NWC item as Days of Revenue or COGS:
  AR (Days Sales Outstanding):    AR / (Revenue / 365)
  Inventory (Days Inventory):     Inv / (COGS / 365)
  AP (Days Payable):              AP / (COGS / 365)

CHANGE IN NWC is what enters the FCF calculation:
  → Increase in NWC = Cash outflow (AR/Inventory building up)
  → Decrease in NWC = Cash inflow (collecting on receivables,
    running down inventory, extending payment terms)

MODELING DISCIPLINE:
  → Use historical DSO/DIO/DPO ratios as the baseline
  → Adjust for known operational changes (new payment terms,
    inventory strategy changes, receivables management)
  → For a growing business: expect moderate NWC build each year
  → Sanity check: NWC as % of revenue should be stable or
    improving (efficiency); deterioration is a quality flag

Example NWC Build (DataMed Analytics):
  DSO target: 52 days (stable, consistent with history)
  DPO target: 41 days (stable)
  Minimal inventory (software business)
  Net NWC ≈ 8.5% of revenue, stable
  → Δ NWC each year ≈ 8.5% × Revenue growth = small annual outflow
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Lesson 3 Quiz
quiz-wrap
Question 1 — Application: A company has Depreciation & Amortization of $45M annually. Its Capital Expenditures for the past three years have been $22M, $19M, and $23M — averaging $21M. What is the most important analytical observation?
A) The company is being capital-efficient by spending less than D&A B) The company appears to be under-investing — CapEx is consistently below D&A, suggesting the asset base is being depleted rather than maintained C) This is a sign of strong free cash flow generation D) No concern — CapEx fluctuates year to year
Answer: B | Explanation: D&A approximates the economic wear-and-tear on a company's productive assets. When CapEx runs persistently below D&A, the company is allowing its asset base to age without full replacement — it is effectively harvesting the business rather than maintaining it. While this boosts near-term free cash flow, it creates a future CapEx requirement that the model must reflect. An analyst who carries forward $21M of CapEx without recognizing this pattern will dramatically overstate long-run free cash flow. The correct response is to investigate whether this reflects a genuinely asset-light shift in the business model or deferred maintenance that will create a future investment spike.

LESSON 4 — FREE CASH FLOW TO THE FIRM (FCFF): BUILDING THE NUMERATOR
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: val_m3_l4
Difficulty: Advanced
Estimated Time: 20 minutes
Prerequisites: Lessons 1–3; Module 1 (Cash Flow Statement)
Feeds Into: Lesson 5 (WACC), Lesson 6 (Terminal Value)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"Free cash flow to the firm is not on any financial statement. It is constructed. Every analyst builds it from components — revenue drives EBIT, EBIT becomes NOPAT, NOPAT absorbs D&A, CapEx, and working capital changes, and what's left is the number that gets discounted. If any step in that construction is wrong, the entire valuation is wrong. Understanding each component — what it represents, why it's included or excluded, and what it would mean if it changed — is the mark of an analyst who actually understands the model, not just the template."

Section 4.1 — Why FCFF, Not Net Income
l-body:
Net income is not discounted in a DCF. This is the single most common conceptual error made by students building their first model. Net income reflects financing decisions (interest expense) and accounting constructs (non-cash charges, accruals) that distort the actual cash available to the business's capital providers. FCFF strips all of this out.
l-formula block:
FCFF vs. NET INCOME — THE CRITICAL DIFFERENCES
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
NET INCOME (why it fails as a DCF input):
  × Includes interest expense → financing-dependent, not operational
  × Excludes CapEx → capital investment is not an expense on income stmt
  × Includes non-cash items (D&A, SBC) → not cash, must adjust
  × Tax is levered → we need unlevered tax to get to NOPAT
  × Affected by accounting choices → not purely economic

FCFF (what we actually want):
  ✓ Starts with EBIT → pure operating performance
  ✓ Tax-effected to NOPAT → removes financing distortion
  ✓ Adds back D&A → non-cash charge, money hasn't left the building
  ✓ Subtracts CapEx → real cash leaving the business for investment
  ✓ Adjusts for Δ Working Capital → cash timing differences
  ✓ Result: unlevered, after-tax, cash-adjusted operating performance
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 4.2 — Building FCFF Line by Line
l-body:
FCFF is built from five inputs. Each has a precise economic meaning that the analyst must understand before the model makes sense.
l-formula block:
FCFF CONSTRUCTION
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  EBIT                              $XXX
  × (1 − Tax Rate)                  × (1 − t)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  = NOPAT                           $XXX
  + Depreciation & Amortization     + D&A
  − Capital Expenditures            − CapEx
  − Change in Net Working Capital   − ΔNWC
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  = FREE CASH FLOW TO THE FIRM      $XXX
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

WHAT EACH ADJUSTMENT MEANS:
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
EBIT × (1-t) = NOPAT:
  → EBIT is the operating profit before financing and tax
  → We tax it at the marginal rate as if the company had no debt
    (this makes the cash flow unlevered — independent of capital
    structure — so it can be discounted at WACC properly)
  → If the company has debt, actual taxes paid are lower (interest
    creates a tax shield), but that tax shield is captured in the
    WACC calculation, not here

+ D&A:
  → D&A is an accounting expense that does not consume cash
  → It was deducted in computing EBIT, so we add it back
  → We already handle the economic reality of asset replacement
    through the CapEx line

− CapEx:
  → The actual cash investment in fixed assets
  → Not on the income statement at all; captured entirely here
  → This is why companies can be profitable and cash-flow negative
    simultaneously: high CapEx investment periods

− ΔNWC:
  → When NWC increases (more AR, more inventory), cash is consumed
  → When NWC decreases, cash is released
  → A growing business almost always builds NWC each year
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LESSON 5 — WACC: COST OF EQUITY (CAPM), COST OF DEBT, CAPITAL STRUCTURE
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: val_m3_l5
Difficulty: Advanced+
Estimated Time: 30 minutes
Prerequisites: Lessons 1–4; Universal Foundation Module 3 (Debt), Module 7 (Risk)
Feeds Into: Lesson 6 (Terminal Value), Lesson 7 (Sensitivity)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"The WACC is the number that makes or breaks a DCF. At Goldman, analysts present their WACC in the first slide of a valuation — before the output — because the entire output depends on it. A 1% change in WACC on a large-cap DCF can shift enterprise value by 15–25%. And yet most junior analysts treat WACC like a formula to plug inputs into, not a judgment about the riskiness of a business. This lesson teaches you to think about WACC the way a portfolio manager does: as an opinion about risk, not a calculation."

Section 5.1 — The Architecture of WACC
l-formula block:
WACC FORMULA
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
WACC = [Ke × (E / (E+D))] + [Kd × (1−t) × (D / (E+D))]

Where:
  Ke      = Cost of Equity
  Kd      = Pre-tax Cost of Debt
  t       = Marginal Tax Rate (for the interest tax shield)
  E       = Market Value of Equity (market cap)
  D       = Market Value of Debt (book value as proxy is acceptable)
  E/(E+D) = Equity weight in capital structure
  D/(E+D) = Debt weight in capital structure

Key Principle: WACC is the opportunity cost for ALL capital providers.
  → Equity investors require Ke → the return they could get elsewhere
    for equivalent risk
  → Debt investors require Kd → but interest is tax-deductible,
    so the true cost of debt to the firm is Kd × (1−t)
  → WACC blends these two costs, weighted by capital structure
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 5.2 — The Cost of Equity: CAPM
l-body:
The cost of equity cannot be directly observed. No contract says equity investors must receive 9.5%. Instead, we use the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) to derive the required return — the return an equity investor demands given how much risk the stock adds to a diversified portfolio.
l-formula block:
CAPM: COST OF EQUITY
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Ke = Rf + β × (Rm − Rf)

Where:
  Rf         = Risk-Free Rate
               → Use 10-year U.S. Treasury yield
               → Represents the return from the riskless baseline
               → As of 2024: approximately 4.0–4.5%

  β (Beta)   = Sensitivity of the stock's returns to market returns
               → β = 1.0: moves exactly with the market
               → β = 1.5: 50% more volatile than market
               → β = 0.7: 30% less volatile than market
               → Source: Bloomberg, CapIQ, or calculated from
                 5 years of weekly returns vs. S&P 500

  (Rm − Rf)  = Equity Risk Premium (ERP)
               → Expected return of the market above the risk-free rate
               → Long-run historical ERP: ~5.5–6.0%
               → Damodaran's current estimate: widely referenced
               → This is a judgment call, not a fact

CAPM EXAMPLE — DataMed Analytics:
  Rf = 4.2%  (current 10-yr Treasury)
  β  = 1.15  (healthcare software: slightly above market)
  ERP = 5.5%
  Ke = 4.2% + 1.15 × 5.5% = 4.2% + 6.3% = 10.5%
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

l-body:
Three judgment calls in the CAPM deserve special attention:
Beta selection: Historical beta is backward-looking. A company that has recently become less cyclical (divested a volatile business unit) or more cyclical (entered a new market) has a "true" beta that diverges from the trailing calculation. Professional analysts often use industry median beta rather than company-specific trailing beta for exactly this reason.
The unlevering and relevering adjustment: When comparing betas across companies with different capital structures, analysts remove the effect of leverage (unlever) before benchmarking and then re-lever to the target capital structure. The Hamada equation governs this: β_unlevered = β_levered / (1 + (D/E) × (1−t)).
Equity Risk Premium: The ERP is not a constant. It varies with market conditions, investor sentiment, and geopolitical risk. Using a stale ERP when market risk perceptions have shifted will produce a cost of equity that does not reflect current required returns.

Section 5.3 — The Cost of Debt
l-formula block:
COST OF DEBT
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Pre-Tax Cost of Debt (Kd):
  Method 1: Current yield on outstanding bonds
    → If DataMed has bonds trading at YTM of 5.8%: Kd = 5.8%
    → Most accurate — reflects actual market rate

  Method 2: Credit rating spread approach
    → Risk-free rate + Credit spread for rating category
    → DataMed (BBB rated): 4.2% + 1.6% spread = 5.8%

  Method 3: Average interest rate on existing debt
    → Interest Expense / Average Debt balance
    → Less preferred — backward-looking, mixes old and new debt

After-Tax Cost of Debt:
  → Kd × (1 − t) = 5.8% × (1 − 24%) = 4.4%
  → Interest expense is tax-deductible; the government subsidizes
    the cost of debt through the tax shield
  → Always use after-tax cost of debt in the WACC formula

DataMed Analytics — Kd(after-tax) = 5.8% × 0.76 = 4.4%
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 5.4 — Capital Structure Weights and the Full WACC Build
l-body:
Capital structure weights are based on market values, not book values. The market value of equity is simply the current market capitalization. The market value of debt is approximated by book value for investment-grade companies where bonds trade near par.
l-example block: Label: FULL WACC BUILD — DATAMED ANALYTICS
Capital Structure (market values): Equity: $2,280M (market cap: 45.6M shares × $50.00/share) Debt: $420M (total debt at book value, near par) Total Capital: $2,700M
Weights: E / (E+D) = $2,280M / $2,700M = 84.4% D / (E+D) = $420M / $2,700M = 15.6%
Cost of Equity (CAPM): Ke = 4.2% + 1.15 × 5.5% = 10.5%
Cost of Debt (after-tax): Kd(at) = 5.8% × (1 − 24%) = 4.4%
WACC: WACC = [10.5% × 84.4%] + [4.4% × 15.6%] = 8.86% + 0.69% = 9.5% (rounded)
Sanity check: For a mid-cap healthcare software company with moderate leverage: 9.5% WACC is consistent with sector benchmarks (8.5%–11.5%) ✓ Reasonable

quiz-wrap
Question 1 — Application: A company has a Beta of 1.30, the risk-free rate is 4.0%, and the Equity Risk Premium is 5.5%. Its pre-tax cost of debt is 6.2%, its marginal tax rate is 25%, and its capital structure is 70% equity / 30% debt by market value. What is the WACC?
A) 9.9% B) 10.15% C) 11.1% D) 8.6%
Answer: A — 9.9%
Ke = 4.0% + 1.30 × 5.5% = 4.0% + 7.15% = 11.15%
Kd(at) = 6.2% × (1 − 25%) = 4.65%
WACC = (11.15% × 70%) + (4.65% × 30%)
     = 7.81% + 1.40% = 9.21% ≈ 9.2%

Question 2 — Judgment: An analyst is building a WACC for a company that is currently 40% debt / 60% equity (market value). The company has publicly stated it will pay down all debt within 3 years, reaching a 0% debt structure. Should the analyst use the current 40% debt weight or the projected 0% debt weight?
A) Current structure — the DCF uses today's cash flows, discounted at today's rates B) Target structure — WACC should reflect the long-run capital structure since terminal value dominates the valuation C) Average of current and target: 20% debt / 80% equity D) Model both and average the WACC outputs
Answer: B | Explanation: WACC represents the long-run required return of the business, and since terminal value typically accounts for 60–80% of the DCF's total enterprise value, the terminal period's capital structure dominates. Using the current high-debt WACC would produce a low WACC that misrepresents the cost of capital during the period that contributes most to value. Analysts typically use the target or long-run capital structure for the WACC when a company has a clearly stated and credible deleveraging path. If the deleveraging is uncertain, analysts will build sensitivity analysis around both scenarios.

LESSON 6 — TERMINAL VALUE: GORDON GROWTH MODEL VS. EXIT MULTIPLE
Lesson Metadata
Lesson ID: val_m3_l6
Difficulty: Advanced+
Estimated Time: 25 minutes
Prerequisites: Lessons 1–5
Feeds Into: Lesson 7 (Sensitivity)

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"Terminal value is where DCF models die. In most mid-cap company DCFs, the terminal value accounts for 65–80% of the total enterprise value. If the terminal value is wrong, the model is wrong — regardless of how precise the five-year forecast is. And yet terminal value is built from two assumptions: a long-run growth rate and a discount rate (or a single exit multiple). An analyst who treats these as plug numbers — 'I'll use 3% growth and 10x EBITDA, that seems reasonable' — has constructed a model where 75% of the output is driven by two arbitrary numbers. This lesson teaches you to think rigorously about what those numbers should be and why."

Section 6.1 — Why Terminal Value Exists
l-body:
A DCF cannot forecast a business indefinitely. After 5–10 years, projections lose analytical credibility — the compounding of assumptions becomes too severe and the uncertainty too wide. But a business does not stop generating cash flows at Year 5. Terminal value is the lump-sum present value of all cash flows the business will ever produce beyond the explicit forecast period.
l-formula block:
TERMINAL VALUE AS % OF TOTAL DCF VALUE — BENCHMARKS
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
High-growth company (SaaS, healthcare tech):
  → TV as % of EV: 70–85%
  → Reason: Most value is in future growth, not near-term FCF

Mature industrial company:
  → TV as % of EV: 55–70%
  → Reason: More near-term FCF; terminal growth rate is lower

Declining/legacy business:
  → TV as % of EV: <50%
  → Reason: Near-term cash flows are the primary value driver;
    long-run prospects are structurally uncertain

Implication for the analyst:
  → The terminal value assumption is not a rounding detail
  → It is the most impactful single input in most DCF models
  → Treat it with the same rigor as the revenue projection
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 6.2 — Method 1: The Gordon Growth Model (Perpetuity Growth)
l-formula block:
GORDON GROWTH MODEL (PERPETUITY GROWTH METHOD)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Terminal Value = FCF_n+1 / (WACC − g)

Where:
  FCF_n+1  = Year 1 FCF beyond the forecast period
             = Last forecast year FCFF × (1 + g)
  WACC     = Discount rate (same as used in projection period)
  g        = Long-run perpetuity growth rate

Key constraints on the growth rate (g):
  → g must be LESS THAN WACC (or the formula produces infinite value)
  → g must be economically sensible long-term:
    Convention: g ≈ long-run nominal GDP growth rate
    Range typically used: 1.5% – 3.5% for U.S. companies
    g > 4.0% implies the company eventually becomes the entire economy
  → g should reflect the terminal state of the business:
    Cyclical commodity business: use 1.5–2.0%
    Stable consumer franchise:   use 2.0–2.5%
    Structural growth sector:    use 2.5–3.5% (max)

DataMed Analytics Example:
  Year 5 FCFF:         $86.6M
  Long-run growth (g): 2.5%
  WACC:                9.5%

  FCF_n+1 = $86.6M × 1.025 = $88.8M
  Terminal Value = $88.8M / (9.5% − 2.5%) = $88.8M / 7.0% = $1,268M
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 6.3 — Method 2: Exit Multiple (EV/EBITDA)
l-formula block:
EXIT MULTIPLE METHOD
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Terminal Value = Terminal Year EBITDA × Exit Multiple

Where:
  Terminal Year EBITDA = Year 5 (or last forecast year) EBITDA
  Exit Multiple        = EV/EBITDA multiple at which the business
                         is assumed to be sold or valued at exit

How to select the exit multiple:
  → Benchmark against current trading multiples of comparable companies
  → Adjust for expected changes in growth profile, margin, or
    competitive dynamics by the terminal year
  → Multiple should reflect terminal business quality — if the
    company will be more mature and slower-growing in Year 5,
    the exit multiple should be lower than current comps
  → Typical ranges: 8x–18x for most industrials and tech/software

DataMed Analytics Example:
  Year 5 EBITDA:        $147.3M
  Exit Multiple:        12.0x (current comps: 11x–14x; conserv. end)
  Terminal Value:       $147.3M × 12.0x = $1,767M

COMPARISON:
  Gordon Growth Model:  $1,268M
  Exit Multiple:        $1,767M
  Gap:                  $499M — significant; requires investigation
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════


Section 6.4 — Reconciling the Two Methods
l-body:
A $499M gap between two terminal value methods is not a tiebreaker problem — it is an analytical signal. The two methods are using different language to make the same statement about value. When they diverge significantly, the analyst must understand why.
l-callout block: Label: THE RECONCILIATION TECHNIQUE
"When my GGM and Exit Multiple produce materially different terminal values, I back into the implied growth rate from the Exit Multiple and the implied multiple from the GGM — and I ask which is more believable. In this case, a 12x EBITDA exit implies a growth rate of approximately 4.4% in perpetuity [backing into g: TV = FCF/(WACC-g) → 1,767 = 88.8/(9.5%-g) → g ≈ 4.5%]. Is 4.5% long-run growth defensible for a mid-cap healthcare analytics company? That's above nominal GDP. Probably not, unless there's a structural reason for above-market growth. The GGM at 2.5% implies a 8.6x EBITDA exit multiple. Is 8.6x fair for a company with 27% EBITDA margins in a growing sector? Probably too low. The 'right' answer is somewhere in between — and the sensitivity table will show exactly how much it matters."
Professional convention: Present both methods. Use the midpoint or a slightly conservative blended estimate in the base case. The sensitivity table (Lesson 7) will then show the range.

Question 2 — Judgment: An analyst's Gordon Growth Model terminal value implies a perpetuity growth rate of 5.8% when reverse-engineered from the Exit Multiple method. The company's sector is growing at 4% annually today. What is the most appropriate response?
A) Accept the exit multiple if it matches peer trading multiples — the market has priced this in B) Flag the implied growth rate as economically implausible; a 5.8% long-run perpetuity rate implies the company will grow faster than the broader economy indefinitely C) Reduce the WACC to bring the methods into alignment D) Extend the forecast period to 10 years before computing terminal value
Answer: B | Explanation: A perpetuity growth rate of 5.8% implies that this company will grow at 5.8% per year forever — indefinitely into the future. For any finite company in any finite market, this is ultimately impossible: a company cannot grow faster than the overall economy in perpetuity without eventually becoming the economy. The fact that the company's sector grows at 4% today does not justify a 5.8% long-run rate because sector growth typically decelerates as the sector matures. The analyst should either (1) reduce the exit multiple to a level consistent with a defensible terminal growth rate, or (2) use a lower GGM growth rate and accept the terminal value it produces, rather than forcing the GGM to match an implausible multiple.

Estimated Time: 20 minutes

Lesson Hook
l-hook block | Label: ANALYST BRIEF
"It does not matter how precise your base-case DCF is. When you present it to an MD, the first question will be: 'Show me the sensitivity table.' Not because they distrust your base case — but because no one trusts a single-point valuation. The sensitivity table is how an analyst earns the right to have a view: you've shown you understand which assumptions matter, how much they matter, and where your conviction ends and uncertainty begins. The analyst who cannot produce a clean, correctly structured sensitivity table on demand has not finished the model."

Section 7.1 — What Sensitivity Analysis Is and Why It Exists
l-body:
A DCF produces a single output: one enterprise value from one set of assumptions. But every assumption in the model is uncertain. Revenue growth might be 6% or 9%. WACC might be 8.5% or 10.5%. The terminal growth rate might be 2.0% or 3.0%. A sensitivity table maps how the output (enterprise value, equity value, or price per share) changes as the most consequential inputs vary.
The MD asks for the sensitivity table first because:
l-list:
It reveals which inputs are actually driving value. If a 1% change in WACC moves the price per share by $8 but a 1% change in revenue growth moves it by only $2, the model is WACC-sensitive — and the analyst should spend more time defending the WACC assumption than the revenue forecast.


It replaces false precision with an honest range. A single DCF output of $52.40 per share implies precision the model cannot deliver. A sensitivity table showing a range of $38–$71 per share depending on WACC and terminal growth rate communicates the appropriate level of analytical confidence.


It frames the investment thesis. If the bear case WACC (10.5%) and conservative growth (5%) produce a price of $38 — still above the current trading price of $34 — the thesis is more compelling than any single base-case number. The sensitivity table provides the margin of safety analysis that every investor cares about.

Lesson 7 Quiz
quiz-wrap
Question 1 — Application: In the DataMed sensitivity table, the current market price of $21.50 is below virtually every scenario. An analyst presents this to her MD and says: "The stock is clearly undervalued — my model shows it's worth at least $21.60 in the worst case." The MD pushes back. Why?
A) The MD thinks the model is too optimistic B) A sensitivity table only shows what the model says — and the model's inputs could themselves be wrong; the analysis shows the stock appears cheap given these assumptions, but the analyst must defend each assumption C) The model should always have scenarios below the market price to be credible D) The analyst should use the exit multiple method instead
Answer: B | Explanation: The sensitivity table is only as good as the assumptions surrounding it. If the WACC range is 8.5%–10.5% and the terminal growth range is 1.5%–3.5%, those ranges themselves represent analyst judgment. If the true WACC should be 12% (because the company has higher risk than the analyst assessed), or the true long-run growth rate is 0% (because the market is maturing), the table would produce entirely different results. The MD is correctly insisting that the analyst defend each input — the sensitivity table shows consequences of assumptions, not justification of assumptions. The analyst's job is not complete until she can defend the range of WACCs and growth rates as the realistic range of outcomes.

Question 2 — Judgment: Two analysts build DCFs for the same company. Analyst A produces a range of $38–$72 per share across a 5×5 sensitivity table. Analyst B produces a range of $50–$55. The current price is $52. Which analyst's model is more useful, and why?
A) Analyst B — a tighter range is more precise and actionable B) Analyst A — a wide range suggests greater analytical rigor and captures the true uncertainty C) Analyst A — only if the wide range reflects genuinely different but defensible input assumptions; a wide range from arbitrary inputs is not valuable D) Neither — a model that includes the current price is not useful for investment decisions
Answer: C | Explanation: A wide sensitivity range is only valuable if the range of inputs reflects genuine uncertainty across defensible assumptions. If Analyst A's range is wide because she varied WACC from 5% to 15% — an arbitrary spread rather than a realistic scenario range — the width is noise, not rigor. If the range is wide because the company genuinely operates in a sector where a 3% variation in growth rate is analytically justifiable (due to TAM uncertainty or competitive dynamics), the wide range correctly communicates model uncertainty. Analyst B's narrow range may indicate model precision — or it may indicate that she used an unrealistically narrow range of assumptions. The useful question is not "how wide?" but "do the input ranges reflect the true uncertainty of the business?"

MD-Level Answer Framework (Rubric)
l-callout block: Label: WHAT SEPARATES A STRONG SUBMISSION FROM A WEAK ONE
"A strong DCF submission does not have a bigger model. It has better-reasoned assumptions. When I review first-year analyst models, I look for three things: First, can I trace every single input to either a historical data point or an explicit, documented judgment? If a margin assumption has no annotation, the analyst doesn't understand why they chose it. Second, does the FCFF conversion make sense for the business type? A capital-light software business should convert 65–75% of EBITDA to FCFF. An industrial manufacturer with significant reinvestment needs should convert 40–55%. If those numbers are wrong, the capital intensity assumptions are wrong. Third, does the analyst read the sensitivity table correctly — meaning, do they understand what the market is implying, and can they articulate the specific assumptions that differ from consensus? A student who can do all three is ready to sit down with a client. A student who cannot is still building models, not doing analysis."

APPENDIX A — COMMON DCF FAILURE MODES
The Nine Ways Analysts Destroy a DCF
1. Discounting Net Income Instead of FCFF The most foundational error. Net income includes interest expense (financing decision), excludes CapEx (investment decision), and is distorted by non-cash items. A DCF built on net income is not a DCF — it is a discounted earnings model with the wrong denominator.
2. Using Book Value for Capital Structure Weights WACC weights must use market values. Equity book value (shareholders' equity on the balance sheet) bears no necessary relationship to market capitalization. A company trading at 3x book will produce a completely wrong WACC if book value equity is used. Always use market cap for the equity weight.
3. Terminal Growth Rate Greater Than Long-Run GDP A perpetuity growth rate above ~3.5% for a U.S. company implies the company will eventually be larger than the entire economy. Analytically indefensible. Models that produce this result (often because an exit multiple implies it) need to be corrected, not accepted.
4. CapEx Equal to D&A in the Projection Period Setting CapEx equal to D&A assumes zero growth investment — only maintenance spending. This is only appropriate for truly declining or run-off businesses. A business growing revenue 7%+ annually must invest in capacity. CapEx must exceed D&A during high-growth periods.
5. Ignoring Working Capital Changes Carrying NWC forward flat while revenue grows 8% per year produces a model where the business generates unlimited cash from every dollar of revenue growth with no working capital consumption. This is only true for a service business with no AR or inventory. For any business with meaningful receivables or inventory, NWC builds proportionally with revenue.
6. Not Cross-Checking Terminal Value Implied Assumptions Building a GGM terminal value and never checking what it implies about exit multiples — or vice versa — means the analyst has no idea whether their terminal value is internally consistent with observable market benchmarks. Always cross-check both methods.
7. Using WACC for a Highly Levered Company Without Adjustment WACC assumes stable capital structure. For a company with 80% debt/capital that plans to deleverage to 30% over five years, the capital structure and therefore the WACC are changing over time. Highly leveraged situations (LBOs, distressed companies) often require Adjusted Present Value (APV) methodology rather than standard WACC-based DCF.
8. Single-Point Output Without Sensitivity A DCF that presents one number without a sensitivity table is not a completed model — it is a model in progress. Every DCF must include sensitivity analysis before it is presented. Non-negotiable.
9. Anchoring to the Current Stock Price The purpose of a DCF is independent valuation. If the analyst iterates until the model produces the current trading price, they have not analyzed the company — they have rationalized the market's judgment. The model must flow from assumptions to value, not from price back to assumptions.
